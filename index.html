C. DIAGNOSTIC ANALYSIS

Why did it happen? Evidence + response actions

Diagnostic Action Board

Filter issues by focus area. This section includes Diagnostic Analytics items 1 to 7.

1. Root Cause Analysis
Enrollment instability and decline

Evidence: Total enrollment decreased by 218 students or -11.1% over five years.

Cause: Irregular student intake in Kinder and Grade 1 plus inconsistent cohort progression.

Response: Monitor intake by grade level every enrollment period and flag grades with large movement.
2. 5 Whys Analysis
Why are fewer students continuing?
  1. Enrollment declines because fewer students continue.
  2. Some families may transfer or migrate.
  3. Preferences may change due to programs, accessibility, and class setup.
  4. The school lacks proactive attraction and retention strategies.
  5. Monitoring and community engagement are limited.
Response: Use exit survey, parent feedback, and early registration monitoring.
3. Comparison Analysis
Grade-level differences show uneven movement

Kinder and Grade 1 fluctuate, Grade 3 and Grade 6 show large declines, while SPED increases.

Response: Compare each grade against previous and next grade to find cohort breaks.
4. Variance Analysis
Largest positive and negative changes

Major declines: Grade 3 (-129 / -35.6%) and Grade 6 (-94 / -33.2%).

Growth: SPED (+34 / +97.1%), Grade 2 (+35 / +17%), Grade 5 (+31 / +14.6%).

Response: Prioritize Grade 3 and Grade 6 retention review; support SPED expansion.
5. Drill-down Analysis
Find where the decline is concentrated

The biggest drops are not across all grades equally. They are concentrated in specific cohorts, especially Grade 3 and Grade 6.

Response: Drill down by section, adviser, transfer records, and completion status.
6. Exception Analysis
Unusual cases that need attention
  • 2023-2024 enrollment spike to 1,914.
  • Grade 6 drop in 2025-2026 to 189.
  • Kinder overcrowding in 2023-2024 at around 60 students per room.
Response: Check exceptional years and rooms before final planning.
7. Pattern Identification
Repeated trends across the data
  • Overall declining enrollment with one recovery year.
  • Unstable early-grade intake.
  • Irregular cohort movement.
  • Growing SPED demand.
  • Stable teacher ratio means capacity is not the main problem.
Response: Connect trend alerts to monthly decision meetings.

Diagnostic Workflow

  2. Compare affected grades against prior years.
  3. Validate with dropout, transfer, repeater, and attendance records.

Predictive Action Board

Includes Predictive Analytics items 1 to 6 with forecast triggers and preparation steps.

1. Enrollment Trend Prediction
Projected decline without intervention

Finding: Enrollment shows an overall declining pattern at around -2% to -4% yearly.

Forecast: 2026-2027: ~1,700; 2027-2028: ~1,650.

Preparation: Start enrollment recovery and community outreach before the next school year.
2. Teacher-Student Ratio
Ratio may remain stable or improve

Finding: Teacher-student ratio remains around 31–32 students per teacher.

Forecast: If enrollment drops but teachers decrease, ratio stays ~31. If teachers remain, ratio may improve.

Preparation: Avoid automatic staff reduction; review actual section needs first.
3. Class Size Distribution
Uneven class sizes may continue

Finding: Most classes are in the 31–40 range, but some reach 41–50 or higher.

Forecast: More uneven distribution and overcrowding risk in selected sections.

Preparation: Use annual section planning and room redistribution.
4. Dropout Rate
Dropout risk can stay low if intervention continues

Finding: Highest recorded dropout rate was 0.38% in 2024-2025; most recent is 0.06%.

Forecast: Can remain low if current support continues; may rise again if monitoring stops.

Preparation: Keep at-risk student tracking and parent follow-up.
5. Repeater Rate
Academic support may stabilize repeaters

Finding: Peak repeater rate was 0.63% in 2023-2024, then decreased.

Forecast: May stabilize with sustained remedial classes and tutoring.

Preparation: Maintain academic intervention logs per grade level.
6. Attendance Rate
Reported 100% attendance needs validation

Finding: Attendance is reported as 100% across years, which may indicate recording limitations.

Forecast: Data may remain unreliable if validation procedures are not implemented.

Preparation: Use verified attendance logs and monthly checking.

E. PRESCRIPTIVE ANALYSIS

What should management do?

Prescriptive Action Board

Complete recommended actions from the report: Quick Wins, Medium-Term Actions, risks, benefits, and timeline.

1. Quick Win • 0–6 Months
Centralized Data Monitoring Dashboard

Actions / Steps: Develop a unified dashboard using Web, Excel, or Google Sheets; consolidate enrollment, attendance, and grade-level data monthly; assign a data coordinator.

Risks / Challenges: Inconsistent data entry; resistance from staff unfamiliar with digital tools.

Benefits: Faster reporting within 1–2 months; improved decision-making within 3 months.
2. Quick Win • 0–6 Months
Attendance and At-Risk Student Tracking System

Actions / Steps: Identify students below 90% attendance; create weekly monitoring and intervention logs; assign teachers to follow up.

Risks / Challenges: Increased teacher workload; lack of parental cooperation.

Benefits: Early dropout identification within 1–2 months; reduced absenteeism within 6 months.
3. Quick Win • 0–6 Months
Monthly Data Review and Decision Meetings

Actions / Steps: Conduct monthly meetings; review enrollment, class size, and SPED growth; implement immediate corrective actions.

Risks / Challenges: Time constraints and possible lack of data-driven culture.

Benefits: Improved coordination within 3 months; data-driven culture within 6 months.
4. Medium-Term • 1–3 Years
Enrollment Recovery and Retention Program

Actions / Steps: Conduct exit surveys; implement targeted retention programs; strengthen early registration and community outreach.

Risks / Challenges: External demographic changes; low parent participation.

Benefits: Stabilized enrollment within 1–2 years; improved retention within 2–3 years.
5. Medium-Term • 1–3 Years
SPED Resource Expansion and Reallocation

Actions / Steps: Reallocate classrooms from declining grade levels to SPED; train or hire SPED-specialized teachers; provide specialized learning materials.

Risks / Challenges: Budget limitations; shortage of trained SPED teachers.

Benefits: Improved SPED service delivery within 1–2 years; better outcomes within 2–3 years.
6. Medium-Term • 1–3 Years
Class Size Optimization and Section Redistribution

Actions / Steps: Maintain 35–45 students per section; merge underutilized classes; expand overcrowded sections; use annual section planning.

Risks / Challenges: Scheduling conflicts; resistance to section reshuffling.

Benefits: Balanced classroom distribution within 1 school year; improved engagement within 2 years.
